How I work

Six things I do before writing a single PRD.

Most PM work goes wrong in the first 48 hours — when a problem is handed off without enough context, or when a feature is scoped before anyone's asked who it's actually for. These are the habits I've built so I don't make those mistakes.

01
Find the friction before the feature.
The best problems aren't in the backlog — they're in the workarounds users have already built. I watch what people do, not what they say they want.
02
Talk to ten users before guessing.
User interviews aren't a phase — they're how I pressure-test every hypothesis. Most of my best decisions came from a quiet contradiction a user mentioned in passing.
03
Frameworks are tools, not theatre.
I use RICE for prioritization, North Star + input metrics for tracking, and Jobs-to-be-Done for scoping. But I'll throw any of them out if the situation needs a different lens.
04
Ship the smallest honest version.
I'd rather ship a 60% solution to ten real users this week than a perfect one to no one next quarter. Real usage data beats every internal debate.
05
Measure what you can defend.
Every metric on my roadmap has a definition document behind it. If I can't explain how it's calculated and what would make it move, I don't put it on a dashboard.
06
Write the post-mortem before launch.
I write down what failure would look like and what we'd learn from it — before shipping. It's the fastest way to surface assumptions a team is making without realizing it.
Case Study 01 Vantedge AI · YC W22 2024 — Present

The trust problem in AI document intelligence.

How we turned a model with >90% accuracy into a product enterprise analysts actually used — by treating trust as a design problem, not a model problem.

The problem I was solving

Vantedge is building AI workflows for enterprise document analysis — the kind of work where a single analyst might spend two days on what the model can finish in minutes. The technical achievement was real: our extraction accuracy crossed 90% early. The product problem was that none of that mattered if analysts didn't trust the output enough to ship it.

When I started shadowing customer workflows, I kept seeing the same pattern: users would run the AI extraction, get a clean structured output, and then redo the work manually to verify it. We'd shipped a time-saving product that wasn't saving any time.

How I found the real friction

I ran a round of 12 user interviews across our enterprise pilot customers, plus screen-recorded workflow sessions with the three heaviest users. I went in expecting to hear about model accuracy — but the friction was somewhere else entirely.

"I believe the model is probably right. I just can't sign off on something I haven't checked myself. If anything's wrong, it's my name on the report." — Senior analyst, pilot customer (paraphrased)

The insight: accuracy wasn't the bottleneck. Verifiability was. Our users weren't refusing to trust the AI — they physically couldn't audit it without re-reading the source document themselves. Every extraction was a black box that required manual re-verification.

The decisions I had to defend

The team's instinct was to push for higher accuracy — keep iterating the prompts, try fine-tuning, add a verification model on top. I argued that more accuracy would not solve a trust problem. Even at 99% accuracy, the analyst still needs to sign their name. The product needed to make verification fast, not chase a moving accuracy ceiling.

I scoped a different bet: invest in trust UX before investing in model gains. Three specific moves, prioritized using RICE against engineering effort and user interview signal:

The trust-UX roadmap
  1. Inline citations. Every extracted field links back to the exact paragraph in the source document. Users can verify in one click instead of re-reading.
  2. Confidence cues. Visual signals (color + score) on each extraction, so users know where to focus their attention. "Spot-check the yellow ones" became a real workflow.
  3. Edit affordances. One-click correction on any extracted value. Edits feed back into a labeled dataset that improves prompts on the next release.

I left explicit model improvements off the roadmap for that quarter. It was the right call but I had to defend it to engineering twice — once at planning, once mid-quarter when the velocity felt slow.

How I measured if it worked

The model evaluation framework I'd built earlier gave us the accuracy signal. But for trust, I defined three new metrics that didn't exist before:

Trust-specific metrics
  • Time-to-verification — how long between extraction and user sign-off
  • Citation click-through rate — proxy for "are users actually using the audit trail?"
  • Edit rate per extraction — too low meant users weren't engaging; too high meant accuracy was the real problem

The combination mattered. High accuracy with low citation click-through would mean users weren't verifying at all (dangerous). High click-through with low edit rate would mean verification was easy and the model was right. That's what we needed.

Under the hood: how the eval rubric actually worked

Talking about "rubric-based scoring" is easy. The thing that made it useful was the specificity — each criterion had to be defendable, and each score had to map to a concrete failure mode an engineer could fix. Here's a representative scoring rubric for a single extraction, the kind we ran against every golden test case.

Criterion
What we check
Score
Accuracy
Does each extracted field match the ground-truth value? Partial credit for near-misses (e.g. rounded numbers, equivalent phrasings).
0–3
Faithfulness
Is every claim in the output traceable to the source document? Any unsupported claim drops the score to 0.
0–3
Citation correctness
Does the cited paragraph actually support the extracted claim? Wrong citations were worse than no citation.
0–2
Completeness
Were all required fields extracted? Penalize silent omissions; an "I don't know" was acceptable, a missing field was not.
0–2
Format adherence
Does the output match the schema we asked for? Strict — downstream code depended on it.
0–1

A passing extraction needed ≥9/11 with no zeros on Faithfulness or Citation correctness. The constraint mattered: a model that scored highly on accuracy but fabricated citations was worse than a model that scored lower but cited honestly. We'd rather ship a humble model than a confident one.

# Example: a single eval case run through the rubric case_id: "contract_termination_clause_017" document_type: "vendor_contract_pdf" expected_field: "termination_notice_period_days" ground_truth: 90 model_output: 90 citation: "Section 12.3, page 4: 'either party may terminate with 90 days written notice...'" scores: accuracy: 3 # exact match faithfulness: 3 # claim fully supported citation: 2 # cited paragraph contains the claim completeness: 2 # field present format: 1 # integer as expected total: 11 / 11 → PASS

The framework ran on every model release across a golden set of ~150 cases spanning document types and edge categories (multilingual clauses, OCR'd scans, tables, footnotes). We caught at least three regressions pre-launch that would have shipped under a "vibes-based" review.

Under the hood: a prompt that took four versions

Our hardest extraction problem was pulling structured data from long, unstructured contracts — 40–80 pages of dense legal prose with information scattered across sections, footnotes, and amendment appendices. The first prompt we shipped looked reasonable. It was wrong in three different ways before we got it right.

The prompts below are representative patterns based on the kind of iteration we did at Vantedge. Exact production prompts are proprietary; what's shown here is the structure and reasoning behind the changes.
v1 — what we shipped first Failed on long docs
Extract the key contract terms from the document below. Return the result as JSON with these fields: - parties - effective_date - termination_notice_period_days - governing_law - payment_terms Document: {{document_text}}
What went wrong: On short documents this worked fine. On 60-page contracts, the model would invent plausible-sounding values when it couldn't find the real ones — a 30-day termination notice that didn't exist anywhere in the contract, governing law guessed from the company's headquarters. Faithfulness collapsed.
v2 — added "I don't know" Reduced hallucinations, but format broke
Extract the key contract terms from the document below. For each field, return: - the extracted value, OR - "NOT_FOUND" if the field is not explicitly stated in the document. DO NOT infer or guess. If unsure, return "NOT_FOUND". Fields: parties, effective_date, termination_notice_period_days, governing_law, payment_terms Document: {{document_text}}
What got better: Hallucinations dropped sharply — the model started returning "NOT_FOUND" instead of inventing. What got worse: The model started returning inconsistent JSON, sometimes wrapping NOT_FOUND in quotes, sometimes not. Downstream parsing broke. We'd traded one bug for another.
v3 — enforced schema + reasoning Better, but slow on long docs
You are extracting structured data from a legal contract. Follow this process for each field: 1. Search the document for the relevant clause. 2. If found, quote the exact source text supporting your answer. 3. Extract the value. If not found, set value to null. 4. Return the result in the JSON schema below. Schema: { "parties": { "value": string | null, "source_quote": string | null }, "termination_notice_period_days": { "value": int | null, "source_quote": string | null }, ... } Document: {{document_text}}
What got better: Forcing the model to quote its source before answering improved faithfulness dramatically — the act of finding the quote was the verification step. JSON schema was strict, parsing was clean. What was still wrong: On 60+ page documents, the model would still miss clauses buried in amendments at the end. The chain-of-thought helped, but the model wasn't searching exhaustively.
v4 — sectioned retrieval + few-shot Shipped to production
You are extracting structured data from a legal contract. The document has been split into sections (Main Agreement, Schedules, Amendments). Review ALL sections before answering. For each field below, follow this process: 1. List every section that may contain relevant information. 2. Quote the exact text supporting your answer (from any section). 3. If multiple sections mention the same field (e.g. amendment overrides main agreement), use the latest in force. 4. If not found in any section, set value to null. Here are two worked examples of correct extraction: {{few_shot_example_1}} {{few_shot_example_2}} Schema: { ... } Document sections: {{sectioned_document}}
What worked: Three changes together. First, we chunked documents by section before passing them in — so the model knew the structure. Second, we added two few-shot examples covering the hardest cases (a clause in an amendment overriding the main agreement, and a field genuinely not present). Third, we made the "amendments override" rule explicit, which was a real source of errors. Faithfulness scores stabilized above the threshold and accuracy on long-document cases jumped meaningfully.

The lesson I carried out of this: most prompt failures aren't prompt problems — they're missing context. v1 wasn't a bad prompt; it just didn't tell the model how the documents were structured. v4 worked because we did the work upfront to give the model what it needed before asking it to be clever.

What I learned

Accuracy is a hygiene metric, not a product metric. Once you cross a usable threshold, users don't care if you go from 91% to 94%. They care whether they can ship the output without anxiety. The PM job in AI is to translate model quality into user confidence.
"I don't know" is a feature, not a failure. Confidence cues that surfaced model uncertainty made users trust the product more, not less — because they could allocate attention instead of double-checking everything.
The hardest part wasn't building it — it was defending the scope. Choosing trust UX over model gains was unpopular for a quarter. The data eventually proved it out, but I learned to invest more upfront in bringing engineering along on the why.
Case Study 02 Vantedge AI · Internal Tool 2025

A problem no one asked me to solve.

How a quiet observation about engineering workflow became an internal product the entire team now relies on — and what it taught me about action bias.

The observation

I wasn't supposed to be working on internal tooling. My remit was the customer-facing product. But sitting in engineering standups for a few weeks, I kept noticing the same friction: every time someone needed to push to dev or staging, they'd context-switch into GitHub, navigate to Actions, find the right workflow, fill in parameters, and wait.

It wasn't a crisis. No one was complaining. But the time tax was real — and more importantly, it broke flow. Engineers would lose 5–10 minutes per deploy, do it 4–6 times a day, and the cumulative effect was that shipping felt heavier than it should.

Why I decided to act

Two reasons I didn't wait for someone to formally ask:

  • The cost of inaction was invisible. No one was going to file a ticket for "deploys feel slow." Friction this low-grade gets normalized. If I waited for it to become a stated problem, it wouldn't become one.
  • The blast radius was contained. An internal tool I scoped wrong wouldn't hurt customers. It was the safest possible context to test a hypothesis without permission.

I gave myself a one-week timebox: talk to engineers, scope the smallest useful version, and have something working. If it didn't earn adoption in a month, I'd kill it.

How I scoped it

I sat down with 8 engineers individually — not to gather requirements but to observe their actual deployment workflow. I asked each of them to walk me through their last three deploys. What I heard wasn't what I expected.

"Honestly the GitHub UI is fine. The thing that kills me is forgetting which branch I deployed last and having to dig through Slack to figure it out." — Senior engineer, paraphrased

The real pain wasn't the deploy interface. It was the lack of visibility into deploy state. Who deployed what to where, when, and was it green? That reframed the product entirely. The console wasn't a faster deploy button — it was a deploy status surface that happened to have a deploy button.

What I shipped
v1 scope (one week)
  1. One-click deploy to dev or staging from a single UI.
  2. Live status board: what's deployed where, by whom, when, build state.
  3. One-click rollback to the previous build.

I explicitly left out: production deploys (too risky for v1), environment provisioning, and any kind of permissions system. Cutting scope was the decision that made the project shippable in a week.

How I measured adoption

I didn't want vanity metrics. The only number that mattered was: do engineers reach for this instead of GitHub by default? I instrumented two things: deploys triggered through the console vs. through GitHub directly, and time-from-merge to deployed.

What I learned

Low-grade friction is the highest-leverage thing to fix. No one will file a ticket for it. The PMs who notice it and act anyway end up shipping the tools their teams won't stop using.
The right problem is rarely the stated one. Engineers thought they wanted a faster deploy UI. What they actually needed was deploy visibility. The user interview is where I caught it; without that step I'd have shipped the wrong product.
Permissionless action has limits — but they're further out than you think. I didn't ask before starting. I did make sure I had a kill-criterion (one month, no adoption, shut it down). That balance is something I still tune.
Case Study 03 Scholar Planet · EdTech 2023 — 2024

A multi-sided product, three growth channels, and an AI content system.

The full cold-start story: launching with no users, building both sides of a marketplace (students + teachers), running three different acquisition motions in parallel, and designing a content supply system that evolved from human-only to AI-augmented with a maker-checker quality gate.

The starting position

When I joined Scholar Planet, the platform had a product but essentially no users — the team was preparing to relaunch the app and needed someone to own product end-to-end before, during, and after that relaunch. There was no behavioral data to mine, no existing user base to interview, and no obvious next feature. The job was to build the engine that would create both at the same time.

Cold-start problems are uncomfortable because every PM instinct breaks. You can't A/B test what you don't have traffic for. You can't validate retention hypotheses without retained users. You can't prioritize a roadmap with no user feedback. The discipline becomes sequencing: knowing which problem to solve first so the next one becomes solvable.

How I sequenced the work

I didn't try to figure out retention features on day one. With no users, that conversation has no signal. Instead, I worked the problem in the order that the data would allow:

The sequencing logic
  1. Get the relaunch right. Revamp the app so the next user who landed on it had a chance of staying. Onboarding, core flows, and UI — fundamentals first.
  2. Open three acquisition channels in parallel. School partnerships (B2B2C), tuition centers (SMB), and individual students via marketing campaigns. Each needed a different motion, but together they gave us learning velocity.
  3. Mine the early data, then talk to the users behind it. Once we had real students and teachers on the platform, I ran user interviews against actual behavior patterns — not guesses.
  4. Build the supply side. Designed a teacher product so teachers could add their own students and assign content — turning teachers into content creators and student-acquisition channels in one move.
  5. Build retention loops. Streaks, leaderboards, and school/tuition dashboards specifically designed to extend the moments where students felt seen and teachers felt in control.
  6. Scale content supply. Started with content from real teachers; later layered AI generation with a maker-checker review pattern to scale without losing quality.

Each step unlocked the next. Without the relaunch, partnerships wouldn't have stuck. Without diverse acquisition, we'd have learned only one user pattern. Without the teacher product, we couldn't have scaled content. Without quality gates, AI would have broken what we built.

Three acquisition channels, three different motions

Running three channels in parallel was a deliberate choice. Each one tested a different hypothesis about who Scholar Planet was actually for, and each one taught us something different about the product.

The three channels
  • Schools (B2B2C). Co-led GTM with the CEO; onboarded 50 Kendriya Vidyalayas through institutional partnerships. Slowest motion but highest LTV — once a school was in, hundreds of students came with it.
  • Tuition centers (SMB). Targeted independent coaching institutes that wanted to give their students structured digital practice. Faster to close than schools, and the teachers were more engaged with the product day-to-day.
  • Individual students (B2C). Launched marketing campaigns targeting students directly — especially those preparing for JEE and NEET. Different acquisition cost, different retention curve, but critical for learning what self-motivated users actually wanted.

The insight that came from running them together: each channel surfaced a different user need. Schools cared about reporting and oversight. Tuitions cared about assignment flexibility. Individual students cared about content depth and exam-specific prep. We couldn't have learned all three by going deep on just one.

Building the supply side: a teacher product

About six months in, a clear gap emerged from interviews: teachers at schools and tuitions wanted to do more than monitor — they wanted to create. Assign their own questions. Run their own quizzes. Track their own students' progress. Without a teacher product, we were treating them as passive supervisors of someone else's content.

"I have my own question bank from years of teaching. If I could give that to my students through this app instead of paper, I would push every student to download it tomorrow." — Tuition center teacher, paraphrased

I scoped and shipped a teacher product where school and tuition teachers could add their students, create custom assignments, and track per-student progress. This turned out to be the highest-leverage feature in the whole case study — because it solved two problems at once:

  • Supply. Teachers became content creators. Every assignment a teacher made was effectively new content on the platform, indexed to a real classroom that would use it.
  • Acquisition. Every teacher who joined brought their students with them. Teachers became a B2B2C acquisition channel inside the SMB and institutional motions.

I also pushed for exam-specific content tracks — JEE and NEET preparation modules — because the individual-student channel was clearly biased toward exam prep. Adding structured JEE/NEET content gave that segment a reason to stay past the first week.

Scaling content: from human-only to AI with a quality gate

The teacher-created content model worked, but it didn't scale evenly. A few highly-engaged teachers produced 80% of the content; most teachers wanted to use the platform without creating from scratch. We needed more content, faster, but the educational stakes were too high to just ship raw AI output — wrong answers on JEE/NEET prep would directly hurt students.

I designed a maker-checker workflow for AI-generated content: the model proposed, a human approved. Specifically:

The maker-checker pipeline
  1. Maker (AI). Generated practice questions, explanations, and answer keys against curriculum tags (subject, topic, difficulty, exam type).
  2. Checker (human teachers). Subject-matter teachers reviewed AI output in a dedicated review interface — approve, edit, or reject. Nothing reached students without a human "approve" stamp.
  3. Feedback loop. Rejected and edited items fed back as signal for which content categories the AI was unreliable on, so we could either improve prompts or restrict generation in those areas.

The decision I had to defend was speed vs. trust. The team wanted the throughput AI offered, but I argued shipping a single wrong answer to a JEE aspirant would do more damage than a month of slower content production. The maker-checker pattern was the compromise: AI for speed on the supply side, humans as the non-negotiable quality gate before anything reached students.

This was my first hands-on experience designing an AI workflow with responsible-AI patterns built in — and a direct ancestor of how I now think about human-in-the-loop systems at Vantedge.

How I measured if it worked

North Star was DAU, but DAU is a lagging indicator on a cold-start product. I tracked leading metrics weekly, segmented by acquisition channel and user role:

  • Streak retention rate — % of students whose streaks survived 7+ days. Direct read on whether the engagement loop was holding.
  • Day-7 retention by channel — separated by school / tuition / individual to see which channels brought stickier users.
  • Teacher activation rate — % of onboarded teachers who created at least one assignment in their first two weeks. Proxy for whether the supply side was healthy.
  • AI content acceptance rate — % of AI-generated items approved by human checkers. Quality signal for the maker-checker system.

I built real-time analytics dashboards to automate weekly reporting — saving the team roughly 8 hours/week and giving us faster signal on which channel, which user role, and which content type was responding to each release.

What I learned

Sequence beats strategy on cold-start products. Knowing what to do first mattered more than knowing what to build. The order — relaunch, multi-channel acquisition, data, teacher product, retention loops, AI content — was the actual product decision. Reverse any two steps and the system would have stalled.
Multi-sided products grow through the side you can't see. Students were the visible user, but the teacher product was what made student acquisition scale. The buyer-facing or creator-facing view of a B2B2C product is almost always the most underrated growth surface.
Speed and trust are not the same tradeoff in every product. Shipping fast on a UI tweak is low-risk; shipping fast on educational content that JEE aspirants will rely on is high-risk. The maker-checker pattern wasn't conservative — it was the right calibration for the stakes. That distinction is what responsible AI means in practice, not as a slogan.
